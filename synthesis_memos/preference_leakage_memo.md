# Synthesis Memo: Preference Leakage — A Contamination Problem in LLM-as-a-Judge
**Source:** Li et al. (2025) — *Preference Leakage: A Contamination Problem in Training LLM-as-a-Judge*
**Author:** Kidus Gashaw | **Date:** 2026-04-30

---

## Core Idea

Li et al. identify and characterize preference leakage: a systematic bias that occurs when the model used to generate training data (the generator) and the model being trained as a judge share training history, model family, or fine-tuning lineage. In this setting, the judge learns to score outputs from its own family higher than outputs from other families — not because the outputs are better, but because the shared training distribution produces stylistic and structural patterns the judge has been implicitly tuned to reward.

The paper demonstrates preference leakage empirically across three settings: same-model judge (judge and generator are identical), same-family judge (different sizes of the same model family), and cross-fine-tuning judge (judge is a model fine-tuned from the same base as the generator). In all three settings, the judge shows inflated agreement with the generator's outputs relative to outputs from a different model family. The effect is smallest in the cross-fine-tuning case but still statistically significant (Cohen's d ≈ 0.3–0.5 across experiments).

The paper's recommended mitigation is cross-family judge selection: when training a preference judge, the generator and judge should come from models with different pretraining lineages (e.g., LLaMA-family generator, Qwen-family judge). The paper shows this eliminates the leakage effect in their experiments.

---

## Design Choice I Disagree With

Section 5.3 recommends cross-family judge selection as a sufficient mitigation for preference leakage, and the paper presents this as a complete solution: "selecting a judge from a different model family than the generator eliminates systematic score inflation." The implication is that family diversity alone is the key design variable.

I agree with the recommendation but disagree with the framing that family diversity is sufficient. The paper does not test a second leakage vector that is present in Tenacious-Bench: judge training data generated by the same model that is also used in the evaluation pipeline.

In the standard leakage scenario Li et al. analyze, the leak path is: Generator A produces training data → Judge fine-tuned on that data → Judge evaluates Generator A's outputs. The fix is to replace Generator A with Generator B (different family) for producing evaluation data.

In the Tenacious-Bench pipeline, there is a second path: Claude Sonnet 4.6 generates the training tasks AND generates the "chosen" preference pair responses. If the judge is then evaluated on Claude-generated tasks with Claude-generated gold-standard outputs, the judge learns to prefer Claude-style responses not only because of model family but because the notion of "correct" was defined by Claude in the first place. This is upstream leakage — leakage at the task definition level, not just the response level.

Li et al.'s cross-family mitigation (use Qwen as judge, Claude as generator) addresses the response-level leakage. It does not address task-definition leakage. The mitigation for task-definition leakage is to hold the evaluation rubric constant and treat the rubric — not any model's output — as the ground truth. This is exactly the design of `scoring_evaluator.py`: the five-dimension rubric is defined by the domain (the Tenacious style guide and bench capacity policies), not by any model's output. No Claude-generated "gold standard" is used in evaluation; the evaluator applies fixed pattern logic to whatever output it receives.

The paper would benefit from distinguishing task-level from response-level leakage. Both are present in real preference-training pipelines and require different mitigations.

---

## How This Informed Tenacious-Bench

**Cross-family model routing was implemented before this paper was read:**
The model rotation policy in Tenacious-Bench — Claude Sonnet 4.6 for generation, Qwen for judging — was arrived at independently from first principles (the 78% vs. 4.2% acceptance rate discrepancy during dry-run testing). Li et al.'s paper validates this design: the dry-run result is a direct empirical observation of preference leakage in action. When Claude judged Claude-generated tasks, it accepted 78% — a clear inflation. When Qwen (cross-family) judged the same tasks, it accepted 4.2%. The paper names the mechanism; the dry run observed it.

**Task-definition leakage prevention:**
The rubric in `scoring_evaluator.py` is derived from the Tenacious operational documents (banned-phrase list, CTA policy, bench capacity commitment protocol), not from any model output. This is the mitigation for the task-definition leakage vector that Li et al. do not explicitly address. Every "correct" score in the preference pairs is computed by the deterministic evaluator, not by asking a model to produce a reference answer.

**Held-out partition isolation:**
Li et al. discuss contamination of the evaluation partition by the training pipeline. In Tenacious-Bench, the held-out partition was sealed before any preference pair construction ran — the 40 held-out tasks were never processed by the preference pair construction script, so no chosen/rejected labels exist for them. The evaluator scores them blind. This protocol follows the paper's recommendation against using evaluation data in any part of the training pipeline.

---

## Limitation

Li et al. measure preference leakage using win rate on preference benchmarks as the outcome variable. The paper does not measure leakage effects on rubric-based compliance evaluation, where the outcome is not preference agreement but pattern-match accuracy.

For Tenacious-Bench, the leakage concern is different from win rate inflation: the risk is not that a Claude judge would prefer Claude-style emails on a 1–5 quality scale, but that it would miss specific compliance failures (a banned phrase in the Claude-style register that looks natural to a Claude judge) that a cross-family judge would catch. Li et al.'s empirical design does not test whether cross-family judges are better at catching compliance violations in domain-specific rubrics — it tests whether they produce less biased win rates on general preference benchmarks. These are related but distinct properties. The paper's mitigation recommendation is adopted in Tenacious-Bench for first-principles reasons validated by the dry-run numbers, but the specific compliance-evaluation variant of leakage remains unvalidated by any published study.
